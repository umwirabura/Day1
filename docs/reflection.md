# Reflection

## Critical Analysis

### Course Learnings

This course fundamentally changed how I think about the relationship between digital design and physical fabrication.

#### Key Realizations

**1. Fabrication is a Design Choice**

Before this course, I viewed fabrication as simply "making what was designed." Now I understand that the fabrication method actively shapes what can be designed. The constraints of 3D printing, laser cutting, or CNC milling aren't limitations—they're design parameters.

**2. Materials Have Agency**

Coming from electronics/IoT, I was used to treating enclosures as afterthoughts. This course taught me that materials actively influence outcomes through their properties, behavior, and constraints. Designing *with* materials rather than *despite* them leads to better results.

**3. Iteration is Research**

Each prototype isn't just a step toward a final product—it's an experiment that generates knowledge. Failed prints taught me more about design tolerances than any textbook could.

### Connections to IoT/Embedded Systems

As an IoT engineer, this course revealed surprising parallels:

| IoT/Embedded Concept | Modeling/Fabrication Parallel |
|---------------------|------------------------------|
| Debugging firmware | Iterating on physical prototypes |
| Circuit tolerances | Fabrication tolerances |
| PCB design rules | Design for manufacturability |
| System integration | Assembly design |
| Power constraints | Material/process constraints |

**Key Connection:** Both fields require designing within constraints while finding creative solutions.

## Ethics & Sustainability Reflection

### Critical Questions Encountered

**"Should everything that can be made, be made?"**

This question resonated deeply. As someone who can now design and fabricate rapidly, I have responsibility to consider:

- **Material waste:** Is this iteration necessary, or am I over-prototyping?
- **Energy consumption:** 3D printing is energy-intensive—when is it justified?
- **Longevity:** Am I designing for disposability or durability?
- **Repairability:** Can this be fixed, or must it be replaced?

### Personal Commitments

Moving forward, I commit to:

1. **Design for disassembly** - making repair/upgrade possible
2. **Minimize material waste** - careful planning before fabrication
3. **Choose sustainable materials** when performance allows
4. **Document and share** - so others can learn without repeating my failures

This statement from Day 1 felt abstract initially. After completing this course, I understand it viscerally. The best designs emerge from a dialogue between digital intent and physical reality—neither can exist in isolation.
